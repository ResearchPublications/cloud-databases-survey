\begin{abstract}
Cloud databases have become the backbone of modern data-driven applications, enabling organizations to store, process, and analyze data at unprecedented scale and elasticity. Over the past seven years (2019--2025), more than 50 survey papers have examined various facets of cloud database technology---spanning cloud-native architectures, hybrid transactional and/or analytical processing (HTAP), serverless databases, vector databases, AI-for-database optimization (AI4DB), data lakehouses, NoSQL/NewSQL systems, and edge--cloud data management. However, the challenges, open problems, and research directions identified in these surveys remain fragmented across disparate communities and publication venues. This paper presents the first \emph{systematic meta-survey} of cloud database research: a survey of surveys that synthesizes findings from carefully selected survey papers using a rigorous methodology grounded in the Kitchenham--Charters systematic literature review protocol. The scattered landscape of cloud database challenges is organized into a structured, dual-theme taxonomy: (1)~\emph{eighteen cross-cutting challenges} that transcend individual system categories, including scalability, consistency trade-offs, security, cost optimization, AI--database convergence, and sustainability; and (2)~\emph{five lifecycle-phase challenges} spanning design, deployment, operation, optimization, and evolution of cloud database systems. The convergence of cloud databases with large language models (LLMs) is further examined as an illustrative case study, and a prioritized research roadmap with short-, medium-, and long-term directions is proposed. By providing a consolidated, thematically organized view of the field, this meta-survey serves as both a comprehensive reference for researchers entering the cloud database space and a strategic guide for practitioners navigating the rapidly evolving landscape of cloud data management.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The cloud database market has experienced explosive growth over the past decade, with global revenues projected to exceed \$200 billion by 2028~\cite{gartner2024dbms}. This growth reflects a fundamental shift in how organizations manage data: from on-premises, monolithic database servers to elastic, globally distributed, cloud-native data platforms that can scale storage and compute independently on demand~\cite{Verbitski2017,Dageville2016}. Major cloud providers---Amazon Web Services (AWS), Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud Platform (GCP), and Alibaba Cloud---now offer dozens of specialized database services, from relational engines like Amazon Aurora~\cite{Verbitski2017} and Azure SQL~\cite{Antonopoulos2019} to purpose-built systems for documents, graphs, time series, key-value pairs, and, most recently, vector embeddings for AI workloads~\cite{Pan2024VectorDB}.

This rapid evolution has attracted significant research attention. A search across major digital libraries reveals that \textbf{more than 50 survey papers} have been published between 2019 and 2025, each examining a particular facet of cloud database technology. These surveys span a wide spectrum of topics: cloud-native database architectures~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}, hybrid transactional/analytical processing (HTAP)~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey}, serverless data management~\cite{Muller2020Serverless}, vector database management systems~\cite{Pan2024VectorDB}, AI-driven database optimization (AI4DB)~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB}, data lakehouses~\cite{Harby2022Lakehouse}, NoSQL and NewSQL systems~\cite{Davoudian2018NoSQL,Kaur2021NoSQLReview}, edge and fog database systems~\cite{Gomes2024EdgeFog}, and many more.

Despite this wealth of individual surveys, the field lacks a \emph{meta-level synthesis}---a comprehensive, systematic effort to consolidate the challenges, open problems, and research directions scattered across these surveys into a unified framework. Each survey naturally focuses on its own domain, leading to a fragmented understanding of the cloud database landscape as a whole. Cross-cutting challenges such as cost optimization, security, consistency trade-offs, and AI--database convergence are discussed in many surveys but from different angles, without a common organizing framework.

\subsection{Contributions and novelty}
\label{subsec:contributions}

This paper addresses the gap by presenting the \textbf{first systematic meta-survey of cloud database research}---a survey of surveys. A rigorous systematic literature review (SLR) protocol based on the Kitchenham--Charters guidelines~\cite{Kitchenham2007} is applied to identify, evaluate, and synthesize cloud database surveys. The contributions of this work are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*}]
    \item \textbf{Systematic identification and classification.} Eight digital libraries are systematically searched, yielding 58 survey papers on cloud databases published between 2019 and 2025, classified by topic, scope, methodology, and venue.

    \item \textbf{Dual-theme challenge taxonomy.} The scattered challenges are organized into a structured taxonomy with two complementary themes:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \emph{Theme~1: Eighteen cross-cutting challenges} that recur across multiple survey domains, including scalability, consistency, security, cost modeling, AI--database convergence, and sustainability.
        \item \emph{Theme~2: Five lifecycle-phase challenges} that map to the stages of designing, deploying, operating, optimizing, and evolving cloud database systems.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{LLM--database convergence analysis.} The rapidly emerging intersection of large language models (LLMs) and cloud databases is examined as an illustrative case study, covering text-to-SQL, vector databases for retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and LLM-based database administration.

    \item \textbf{Research roadmap.} A prioritized research roadmap with short-term (1--3 years), medium-term (3--5 years), and long-term (5+ years) directions is proposed.

    \item \textbf{Knowledge engineering perspective.} Cloud database challenges are explicitly connected to data and knowledge engineering concerns---including knowledge graphs, ontologies, metadata management, and schema evolution---making this survey particularly relevant to the \emph{Data \& Knowledge Engineering} community.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Why Cloud Databases Matter---A Multi-Perspective Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Understanding the significance of cloud databases requires examining the topic from multiple viewpoints. In this section, the need for a meta-survey is motivated by discussing five complementary perspectives, each revealing why the scattered state of survey knowledge is problematic and why a unified synthesis is timely.

\subsection{Industry perspective}
\label{subsec:industry}

The global database management system (DBMS) market reached \$101 billion in 2023, with cloud deployments accounting for over 65\% of total revenue---up from approximately 40\% in 2019~\cite{gartner2024dbms}. Gartner projects that by 2026, 75\% of all databases will be deployed on cloud platforms~\cite{gartner2024dbms}. This shift is driven by digital transformation initiatives across industries, from financial services adopting cloud databases for real-time fraud detection to healthcare organizations leveraging cloud data platforms for genomics and precision medicine~\cite{AmazonAurora2023}.

The vendor landscape has also diversified significantly. Beyond the hyperscale cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP), specialized cloud database vendors such as Snowflake~\cite{Dageville2016}, Databricks~\cite{Armbrust2021Lakehouse}, CockroachDB~\cite{Taft2020Cockroach}, PlanetScale, and Neon have gained substantial market share. China's cloud database ecosystem---including Alibaba PolarDB~\cite{Cao2021PolarDB}, TiDB~\cite{Huang2020TiDB}, and OceanBase~\cite{Yang2022OceanBase}---has matured rapidly. This fragmentation makes it difficult for practitioners to navigate the landscape without a systematic overview.

\subsection{Data engineering perspective}
\label{subsec:dataeng}

Modern data architectures have evolved from simple transactional databases to complex, multi-layered data ecosystems. The emergence of the \emph{data lakehouse} paradigm~\cite{Armbrust2021Lakehouse} exemplifies this evolution, combining the flexibility of data lakes with the governance and performance of data warehouses. Systems like Databricks Delta Lake, Apache Iceberg, and Apache Hudi have created a new ``open table format'' layer that bridges batch and streaming analytics~\cite{Jainaga2023Lakehouse}.

Simultaneously, the rise of \emph{HTAP} (Hybrid Transactional/Analytical Processing) systems~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey} challenges the traditional separation between OLTP and OLAP workloads. Systems such as TiDB~\cite{Huang2020TiDB}, SAP HANA~\cite{Farber2012}, and Google AlloyDB promise unified engines that handle both transaction processing and analytical queries. Understanding the trade-offs, maturity, and open challenges of these paradigms requires synthesizing insights across multiple survey papers.

\subsection{Knowledge engineering perspective}
\label{subsec:ke}

Cloud databases play a crucial role in knowledge engineering, making this topic directly relevant to the \emph{Data \& Knowledge Engineering} (DKE) community. Several connections are worth highlighting:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Knowledge graphs in the cloud.} Large-scale knowledge graphs (KGs) such as Google Knowledge Graph, Wikidata, and enterprise KGs are increasingly stored and queried using cloud-hosted graph databases (e.g., Amazon Neptune, Azure Cosmos DB with Gremlin API, Neo4j Aura)~\cite{Angles2020GraphDB}. The scalability, consistency, and query optimization challenges of cloud graph databases directly impact KG applications.

    \item \textbf{Ontology-mediated data access.} Cloud environments with heterogeneous data sources benefit from ontology-based data integration (OBDI) techniques~\cite{Xiao2018OBDA}, where ontologies provide a unified conceptual layer over distributed cloud databases.

    \item \textbf{Metadata and schema management.} Cloud data catalogs and metadata management systems (e.g., AWS Glue Data Catalog, Apache Atlas) implement knowledge engineering principles for organizing, discovering, and governing data assets~\cite{Halevy2016Goods}.

    \item \textbf{AI--knowledge engineering convergence.} The integration of machine learning with database systems (AI4DB)~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB} draws on knowledge engineering concepts such as learned models for query optimization, workload characterization, and automated tuning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Economic and cost perspective}
\label{subsec:cost}

Cloud databases introduce novel economic dynamics compared to on-premises deployments. The shift from capital expenditure (CapEx) to operational expenditure (OpEx) models, combined with pay-per-use pricing and complex multi-dimensional billing (compute, storage, I/O, network egress, backups), creates significant challenges in cost prediction and optimization~\cite{Leis2023CloudPricing}.

Research has shown that cloud database costs can vary by orders of magnitude depending on configuration choices, query patterns, and pricing model selection~\cite{Leis2023CloudPricing}. The total cost of ownership (TCO) analysis becomes particularly complex for multi-cloud or hybrid cloud deployments. Several surveys touch on cost-related challenges~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey,Muller2020Serverless}, but a consolidated analysis is lacking.

\subsection{Regulatory and compliance perspective}
\label{subsec:regulatory}

Data sovereignty regulations such as the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)~\cite{GDPR2016}, China's Data Security Law, and sector-specific requirements (e.g., HIPAA for healthcare, PCI-DSS for payment data) impose strict constraints on how and where cloud databases store and process data~\cite{Politou2018GDPR}.

Multi-region deployment, data residency requirements, cross-border data transfer restrictions, and audit trail obligations all impact cloud database architecture and operations. The compliance landscape interacts with technical challenges such as geo-distributed consistency, encryption, access control, and data lineage---topics discussed in various surveys but rarely addressed in an integrated manner.

\section{From Cloud Databases to Cloud-Native Data Platforms}
\label{sec:concepts}

Before analyzing the surveyed literature, key concepts, definitions, and distinctions are established that form the vocabulary of cloud database research.

\subsection{Cloud-hosted vs.\ cloud-native vs.\ cloud-born databases}
\label{subsec:cloudtypes}

The term ``cloud database'' encompasses a spectrum of architectures with fundamentally different design philosophies. Three categories are distinguished, compared in Table~\ref{tab:cloudtypes}:


\textbf{Cloud-hosted databases} are traditional database engines (e.g., PostgreSQL, MySQL, Oracle) deployed on cloud virtual machines. They benefit from cloud infrastructure (networking, storage volumes, automated backups) but do not exploit cloud-native primitives such as elastic compute pools or disaggregated storage~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Cloud-native databases} are existing or new engines that have been substantially re-architected to leverage cloud services. Amazon Aurora~\cite{Verbitski2017} exemplifies this approach: it re-implements the storage layer of MySQL/PostgreSQL to use a distributed, log-structured storage service while keeping the SQL engine largely intact. Microsoft's Socrates~\cite{Antonopoulos2019} takes SQL Server and disaggregates its components across cloud microservices.

\textbf{Cloud-born databases} are designed entirely from the ground up for cloud deployment, with no legacy on-premises heritage. Snowflake~\cite{Dageville2016} pioneered this model for data warehousing, while CockroachDB~\cite{Taft2020Cockroach} and Neon represent cloud-born approaches for transactional and PostgreSQL-compatible workloads, respectively.

\subsection{Database-as-a-Service (DBaaS) spectrum}
\label{subsec:dbaas}

Database-as-a-Service (DBaaS) represents the fully managed end of the cloud database spectrum, where the cloud provider handles provisioning, patching, scaling, backup, and high availability~\cite{Curino2011DBaaS}. The DBaaS model has evolved along several dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Managed databases}: Provider-managed instances of open-source or commercial engines (e.g., Amazon RDS, Azure Database for PostgreSQL).
    \item \textbf{Serverless databases}: Fully elastic databases that scale to zero when idle and automatically provision resources based on demand (e.g., Amazon Aurora Serverless, Azure Cosmos DB serverless, Neon)~\cite{Muller2020Serverless}.
    \item \textbf{Autonomous databases}: Self-driving databases that use ML to automate tuning, indexing, partitioning, and anomaly detection (e.g., Oracle Autonomous Database)~\cite{Pavlo2017SelfDriving}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Shared-nothing} architectures partition data across independent nodes, each with its own CPU, memory, and disk. Traditional distributed databases (e.g., early Spanner~\cite{Corbett2013Spanner}, Cassandra) follow this model. While horizontally scalable, resharding and rebalancing are expensive.

\textbf{Shared-disk (cloud storage)} architectures decouple compute from storage, allowing compute nodes to share a common cloud storage layer. Amazon Aurora~\cite{Verbitski2017} and Snowflake~\cite{Dageville2016} exemplify this paradigm, enabling independent scaling of compute and storage.

\textbf{Fully disaggregated} architectures further decompose the database engine into independently scalable microservices---query processing, transaction management, logging, metadata management, and storage~\cite{Antonopoulos2019}. Microsoft Socrates and emerging systems like PolarDB~\cite{Cao2021PolarDB} pursue this vision.

\section{Systematic Review Planning and Execution}
\label{sec:methodology}

The systematic literature review (SLR) guidelines established by Kitchenham and Charters~\cite{Kitchenham2007}, which have been widely adopted in software engineering and computer science~\cite{Salehi2016Granular,Murtaza2020BreastCancer,Qazi2015SolarANN,Saeed2023XAI}, are followed. This protocol prescribes a three-phase process: (1)~\emph{planning}, in which research questions, search strategy, and inclusion/exclusion criteria are defined; (2)~\emph{conducting}, in which studies are systematically identified, selected, and their data extracted; and (3)~\emph{reporting}, in which the synthesized findings are documented and validated through quality assessment.

\section{Discussion---Challenges and Research Directions}
\label{sec:discussion}

Following the dual-theme approach of Saeed and Omlin~\cite{Saeed2023XAI}, the discussion is organized into two complementary themes:

\begin{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Scalability and elasticity}
\label{subsubsec:scalability}

\textbf{Context.} Scalability---the ability to handle growing workloads by adding resources---and elasticity---the ability to dynamically provision and release resources in response to demand fluctuations---are foundational requirements for cloud databases~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey,Davoudian2018NoSQL}.

\textbf{Current state.} Cloud-native databases like Aurora~\cite{Verbitski2017} and Snowflake~\cite{Dageville2016} achieve storage elasticity through disaggregation, while compute elasticity is provided by auto-scaling mechanisms. Serverless databases~\cite{Muller2020Serverless} push elasticity further by scaling to zero. However, surveys report that true fine-grained elasticity---scaling individual components (e.g., a single query operator) rather than entire instances---remains an open challenge~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cold-start latency in serverless databases undermines real-time elasticity~\cite{Muller2020Serverless}.
    \item State management during scale-out/scale-in operations can cause transient performance degradation.
    \item Predictive autoscaling---anticipating workload spikes before they occur---requires accurate workload forecasting models~\cite{Ma2021LearnedWorkload}.
    \item Cross-component elasticity in disaggregated architectures (independently scaling query processing, transaction management, and storage) is poorly understood.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} Developing formal models for \emph{elastic data architectures}---capturing the relationships between workload patterns, resource allocation, performance guarantees, and cost---is a knowledge engineering challenge that could benefit from ontological modeling of cloud database configurations.

\subsubsection{Consistency, availability, and partition tolerance revisited}
\label{subsubsec:consistency}

\textbf{Context.} The CAP theorem~\cite{Brewer2012CAP} and its refinement PACELC~\cite{Abadi2012PACELC} have long shaped distributed database design. In the cloud era, these trade-offs take on new dimensions due to geo-distribution, multi-region deployment, and the diversity of consistency models offered by cloud databases~\cite{Viotti2016Consistency}.

\textbf{Current state.} Cloud databases offer a spectrum of consistency levels. Google Spanner~\cite{Corbett2013Spanner} provides external consistency (linearizability) using TrueTime, while Cosmos DB offers five consistency levels from strong to eventual. Surveys on distributed databases~\cite{Davoudian2018NoSQL,Kaur2021NoSQLReview} and HTAP systems~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey} consistently identify consistency trade-offs as a central challenge.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Developers struggle to reason about the implications of different consistency levels for application correctness~\cite{Viotti2016Consistency}.
    \item Adaptive consistency---dynamically adjusting consistency levels based on workload requirements---is proposed but rarely implemented in production systems.
    \item Cross-model consistency in multi-model databases (e.g., ensuring consistency between document and graph views of the same data) is underexplored~\cite{Lu2019MultiModel}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} Formal verification and knowledge-based reasoning about consistency guarantees, including ontology-based specifications of consistency contracts, could help bridge the gap between theoretical models and practical developer needs.

\subsubsection{Performance optimization and benchmarking}
\label{subsubsec:performance}

\textbf{Context.} Performance optimization in cloud databases spans query optimization, indexing, caching, resource allocation, and workload management. Benchmarking provides the empirical foundation for evaluating and comparing systems~\cite{Rabl2019BenchmarkSurvey}.

\textbf{Current state.} Surveys on AI4DB~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB} and cloud-native databases~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey} highlight significant progress in learned query optimization~\cite{Marcus2021Bao}, learned indexes~\cite{Kraska2018LearnedIndex}, and automated knob tuning~\cite{Zhang2022CDBTune}. However, cloud-specific factors---network latency, storage disaggregation overhead, multi-tenant interference, and cold-start effects---complicate traditional optimization approaches. Existing benchmarks (TPC-C, TPC-H, YCSB) are criticized for not capturing cloud-specific characteristics~\cite{Rabl2019BenchmarkSurvey}.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cloud-specific benchmarks that account for elasticity, multi-tenancy, pricing, and variable network performance are lacking.
    \item Fair cross-system comparison is difficult due to differing cloud provider pricing, hardware, and service-level agreements.
    \item End-to-end performance in disaggregated architectures involves complex interactions between independently scaled components.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} A \emph{benchmark knowledge base} that formalizes benchmark configurations, workload characteristics, hardware specifications, and results using structured knowledge representations would enable reproducible and comparable cloud database evaluations.

\subsubsection{Security and privacy}
\label{subsubsec:security}

\textbf{Context.} Cloud databases introduce unique security challenges compared to on-premises systems: shared infrastructure, network-based access, provider trust, and the expanded attack surface of distributed systems~\cite{Fuhrt2010CloudSecurity}.

\textbf{Current state.} Surveys on cloud database security~\cite{Fuhrt2010CloudSecurity,Sanka2022CloudSecSurvey} cover encryption at rest and in transit, access control, audit logging, and vulnerability management. Advanced topics include homomorphic encryption for query processing on encrypted data~\cite{Acar2018HE}, secure multi-party computation, and confidential computing (e.g., Intel SGX enclaves for database operations).

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Performance overhead of encryption, especially for analytical workloads, remains prohibitive.
    \item Key management across multi-cloud and hybrid deployments is complex and error-prone.
    \item Insider threat models for cloud providers (the ``honest but curious'' provider) are difficult to mitigate without significant performance penalties.
    \item Privacy-preserving analytics that comply with GDPR's right to erasure while maintaining data utility.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} \emph{Privacy ontologies} that formally model data sensitivity, access policies, and regulatory requirements could enable automated compliance checking and policy enforcement across cloud database deployments.

\subsubsection{Cost modeling and optimization}
\label{subsubsec:cost}

\textbf{Context.} Cloud databases employ complex pricing models combining compute hours, storage capacity, I/O operations, network transfer, backup storage, and premium features (encryption, audit logs, multi-region replication)~\cite{Leis2023CloudPricing}.

\textbf{Current state.} Recent studies~\cite{Leis2023CloudPricing} demonstrate that cloud database costs can vary by 10--100$\times$ depending on configuration choices. Surveys on serverless databases~\cite{Muller2020Serverless} and cloud-native systems~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey} identify cost as a critical concern but offer limited quantitative analysis.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cost estimation for complex analytical queries before execution is unreliable.
    \item Multi-objective optimization (cost vs.\ performance vs.\ consistency) lacks mature frameworks.
    \item Reserved vs.\ on-demand vs.\ spot instance strategies for database workloads are poorly studied.
    \item Vendor lock-in makes cost comparison across providers difficult.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} Developing formal \emph{cost knowledge models}---ontologies capturing pricing structures, workload profiles, and optimization strategies---could enable intelligent, automated cost optimization across cloud database services.

\subsubsection{Multi-tenancy and resource isolation}
\label{subsubsec:multitenancy}

\textbf{Context.} Multi-tenancy allows cloud providers to serve multiple customers from shared infrastructure, reducing costs and improving resource utilization. However, it introduces challenges in performance isolation, data security, and fair resource allocation~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Current state.} Cloud-native databases implement multi-tenancy at different levels: shared-nothing (dedicated instances per tenant), shared-process (multiple tenants in one engine with logical isolation), and shared-table (tenants share physical tables with row-level security). Surveys report that noisy neighbor effects---where one tenant's workload degrades another's performance---remain a significant operational challenge~\cite{Narasayya2015MultiTenant}.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Achieving strong performance isolation without dedicated hardware is difficult.
    \item Workload-aware resource scheduling that adapts to heterogeneous tenant workloads.
    \item Balancing isolation guarantees with resource efficiency and cost.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Data model diversity and multi-model integration}
\label{subsubsec:multimodel}

\textbf{Context.} The proliferation of specialized data models (relational, document, graph, key-value, wide-column, vector, time-series) in cloud environments creates challenges for applications that need to query across multiple models~\cite{Lu2019MultiModel}.

\textbf{Current state.} Multi-model databases like Azure Cosmos DB, ArangoDB, and SurrealDB offer multiple data model interfaces over a single backend. Surveys on NoSQL~\cite{Davoudian2018NoSQL} and multi-model systems~\cite{Lu2019MultiModel} report growing adoption but highlight limitations in cross-model query optimization and unified transaction semantics.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Unified query languages that span relational, graph, document, and vector operations.
    \item Cross-model query optimization---optimizing queries that combine, e.g., graph traversals with relational joins and vector similarity search.
    \item Schema mapping and data transformation between models in heterogeneous environments.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} \emph{Knowledge representation in multi-model environments}---using ontologies to provide a unified conceptual layer over diverse data models---is a direct DKE opportunity.

\subsubsection{Query processing across heterogeneous engines}
\label{subsubsec:queryprocessing}

\textbf{Context.} Modern cloud data architectures often involve multiple specialized database engines (OLTP, OLAP, graph, search, vector) that must be queried in concert. Federated query processing and data virtualization are key enablers~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey}.

\textbf{Current state.} Systems like Presto/Trino, BigQuery Omni, and AWS Lake Formation provide federated query capabilities. HTAP surveys~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey} discuss the challenges of routing queries to appropriate engines. However, cross-engine optimization remains primitive---typically limited to simple predicate pushdown.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cost-based optimization across heterogeneous engines with different cost models and capabilities.
    \item Maintaining transactional guarantees across engine boundaries.
    \item Semantic understanding of data across engines to enable intelligent query routing.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} \emph{Ontology-mediated query answering (OMQA)}~\cite{Xiao2018OBDA} can provide a principled framework for querying heterogeneous cloud database engines through a unified conceptual schema.

\subsubsection{Storage disaggregation and log-as-database}
\label{subsubsec:disaggregation}

\textbf{Context.} Storage disaggregation---separating compute from storage into independently scalable layers---is a defining architectural trend in cloud databases. The ``log-as-database'' pattern, pioneered by Aurora~\cite{Verbitski2017}, pushes intelligence into the storage layer~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Current state.} Aurora's innovation of making the redo log the ``database'' reduced write amplification and enabled fast failover. Systems like PolarDB~\cite{Cao2021PolarDB} and Socrates~\cite{Antonopoulos2019} further disaggregate components. Surveys~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey} report that disaggregation simplifies operations but introduces challenges in remote I/O latency, cache coherence, and log management.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Minimizing the performance impact of remote storage access (network vs.\ local SSD latency gap).
    \item Cache management across compute and storage layers in disaggregated architectures.
    \item Log management at scale---compaction, garbage collection, and efficient replay.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Transaction processing in distributed cloud environments}
\label{subsubsec:transactions}

\textbf{Context.} Distributed transaction processing in cloud databases must balance ACID guarantees, performance, and scalability. The design space ranges from strongly consistent protocols (2PC, Paxos/Raft-based) to relaxed models (eventual consistency, CRDTs)~\cite{Corbett2013Spanner,Taft2020Cockroach}.

\textbf{Current state.} Google Spanner~\cite{Corbett2013Spanner} demonstrated that globally distributed strongly consistent transactions are feasible, using synchronized clocks (TrueTime). CockroachDB~\cite{Taft2020Cockroach} and YugabyteDB provide similar guarantees using hybrid logical clocks. NewSQL surveys and HTAP surveys~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey} report that combining strong consistency with high throughput for mixed workloads remains challenging.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reducing commit latency for geo-distributed transactions without sacrificing consistency.
    \item Efficient conflict resolution for concurrent transactions in multi-region deployments.
    \item Transaction processing for mixed HTAP workloads without mutual interference.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Fault tolerance, recovery, and high availability}
\label{subsubsec:faulttolerance}

\textbf{Context.} Cloud databases must tolerate hardware failures, network partitions, availability zone outages, and even entire region failures while maintaining data durability and minimizing downtime~\cite{Verbitski2017,Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Current state.} Modern cloud databases achieve high availability through multi-AZ replication (typically 3-way) with automated failover. Aurora~\cite{Verbitski2017} uses a quorum-based protocol across 6 replicas in 3 AZs. Surveys report that recovery time objectives (RTO) of seconds and recovery point objectives (RPO) of zero are achievable for single-region deployments but remain challenging for multi-region configurations.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multi-region disaster recovery with near-zero RPO and RTO.
    \item Consistent recovery in disaggregated architectures where state is spread across components.
    \item Chaos engineering and fault injection frameworks specific to cloud databases.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Data migration and cross-cloud portability}
\label{subsubsec:migration}

\textbf{Context.} Organizations frequently need to migrate databases between on-premises and cloud, between cloud providers, or between different cloud database services. Migration surveys~\cite{Mathew2021CloudMigration} highlight the complexity of maintaining data consistency, minimizing downtime, and handling schema transformations during migration.

\textbf{Current state.} Cloud providers offer migration services (AWS DMS, Azure Database Migration Service, GCP Database Migration Service), but migrations remain error-prone and labor-intensive. Schema and data model differences between source and target systems are a major pain point. Vendor lock-in---through proprietary APIs, data formats, and features---further complicates portability~\cite{Opara2017LockIn}.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Zero-downtime migration for large databases with continuous write workloads.
    \item Automated schema mapping and transformation between heterogeneous systems.
    \item Standardized portability layers that abstract provider-specific features.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} \emph{Knowledge-based schema mapping}---using ontological representations of schema semantics to automate migration---connects directly to DKE's core strengths in schema integration and data exchange.

\subsubsection{Edge--cloud data continuum}
\label{subsubsec:edgecloud}

\textbf{Context.} The proliferation of IoT devices, autonomous vehicles, and smart city infrastructure has created demand for database capabilities at the network edge, working in concert with cloud databases~\cite{Gomes2024EdgeFog}.

\textbf{Current state.} Edge and fog database surveys~\cite{Gomes2024EdgeFog} identify architectures for distributing data processing between edge devices, fog nodes, and cloud backends. Systems like Azure SQL Edge, AWS Outposts, and CockroachDB's locality-aware features address parts of this challenge.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Data consistency across the edge--fog--cloud continuum with intermittent connectivity.
    \item Intelligent data placement---deciding which data to cache, replicate, or move between edge and cloud.
    \item Resource-constrained query processing on edge devices with limited compute and memory.
    \item Privacy-preserving data aggregation at the edge before cloud upload.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{AI--database convergence (AI4DB and DB4AI)}
\label{subsubsec:ai4db}

\textbf{Context.} The convergence of AI and databases manifests in two complementary directions: AI4DB (using AI to optimize database systems) and DB4AI (using database techniques to support AI/ML workloads)~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB}.

\textbf{Current state.} AI4DB surveys~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB} document progress in learned query optimization~\cite{Marcus2021Bao}, learned indexes~\cite{Kraska2018LearnedIndex}, automated knob tuning~\cite{Zhang2022CDBTune}, learned cardinality estimation, and workload forecasting~\cite{Ma2021LearnedWorkload}. DB4AI encompasses in-database ML (e.g., Google BigQuery ML, Amazon Redshift ML) and optimized data serving for training pipelines.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Deploying learned components in production cloud databases with safety guarantees (graceful degradation when ML models fail).
    \item Training data management for learned database components---how to collect, curate, and update training data from production workloads.
    \item Transfer learning across database instances, workloads, and hardware configurations.
    \item Explainability of AI-driven database decisions (why did the optimizer choose this plan?).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} \emph{Knowledge engineering for self-driving databases}---integrating domain knowledge about database internals with learned models---represents a promising approach to building more robust and explainable autonomous database systems.

\subsubsection{Serverless and autonomous database operations}
\label{subsubsec:serverless}

\textbf{Context.} Serverless databases abstract away all infrastructure management, automatically provisioning resources, scaling, patching, and tuning without human intervention. Autonomous databases go further by using ML to self-diagnose and self-heal~\cite{Pavlo2017SelfDriving,Muller2020Serverless}.

\textbf{Current state.} Aurora Serverless v2, Azure Cosmos DB serverless tier, Neon, and PlanetScale offer serverless interfaces. Oracle Autonomous Database represents the most ambitious autonomous database initiative. Surveys~\cite{Muller2020Serverless} report that serverless models are well-suited for variable workloads but face challenges with sustained high-throughput workloads and cold-start latency.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Achieving predictable performance under serverless autoscaling.
    \item Cost--performance trade-off transparency in serverless models.
    \item Full-stack autonomy: integrating self-tuning, self-healing, and self-securing capabilities.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Data governance, provenance, and lineage}
\label{subsubsec:governance}

\textbf{Context.} As cloud data ecosystems grow in complexity, tracking data origin (provenance), transformation history (lineage), and enforcing governance policies becomes critical for compliance, debugging, and trust~\cite{Halevy2016Goods}.

\textbf{Current state.} Data governance surveys identify the need for metadata management, data cataloging, access control, data quality monitoring, and regulatory compliance. Cloud-native governance tools (AWS Glue Data Catalog, Azure Purview, Google Dataplex) provide partial solutions, but surveys report fragmentation and lack of standardization.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item End-to-end data lineage across heterogeneous cloud services and processing engines.
    \item Automated compliance checking against evolving regulatory requirements.
    \item Provenance-aware query processing that tracks how results are derived.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Research directions.} \emph{Knowledge graphs for metadata management}---using KG technology to represent, link, and reason about metadata across cloud data assets---is a natural DKE contribution.

\subsubsection{Sustainability and energy efficiency}
\label{subsubsec:sustainability}

\textbf{Context.} Data centers consumed an estimated 1--1.5\% of global electricity in 2023, with projections of 3--4\% by 2030 due to AI and cloud growth~\cite{IEA2024DataCenters}. Cloud databases, as heavy consumers of compute and storage resources, have a direct environmental impact.

\textbf{Current state.} Green computing surveys identify opportunities for energy-efficient query processing, workload scheduling to exploit renewable energy availability, and carbon-aware data placement. Major cloud providers now report carbon footprint metrics (AWS Customer Carbon Footprint Tool, Google Cloud Carbon Footprint), but database-level energy optimization is nascent.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Energy-proportional database systems that consume resources proportional to actual workload.
    \item Carbon-aware data placement and replication---favoring regions with cleaner energy grids.
    \item Benchmarking frameworks that include energy and carbon metrics alongside performance.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Standardization and interoperability}
\label{subsubsec:standardization}

\textbf{Context.} The proliferation of cloud database systems, query languages, APIs, and data formats creates an interoperability crisis. While SQL remains the lingua franca for relational databases, graph databases alone use multiple query languages (Cypher, SPARQL, Gremlin, GQL), and NoSQL systems have proprietary APIs~\cite{Angles2020GraphDB}.

\textbf{Current state.} The emergence of GQL (Graph Query Language) as an ISO standard, the SQL/PGQ extension for property graphs in SQL, and open table formats (Iceberg, Delta Lake, Hudi) represent progress toward standardization. However, surveys report that cloud vendors continue to differentiate through proprietary extensions, making true interoperability elusive.

\textbf{Open problems.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Universal query interfaces that span relational, graph, document, and vector operations.
    \item Standardized cloud database APIs for provisioning, monitoring, and management.
    \item Open data formats and protocols to reduce vendor lock-in.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Design phase}
\label{subsubsec:design}

The design phase encompasses decisions made before a cloud database is deployed, including schema design, data model selection, workload characterization, and compliance architecture.

\textbf{Schema design for cloud-native environments.} Traditional schema design principles (normalization, ER modeling) must be adapted for cloud databases where denormalization may improve performance due to the cost of distributed joins~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}. Document and wide-column stores require fundamentally different schema design approaches centered on access patterns rather than entity relationships~\cite{Davoudian2018NoSQL}.

\textbf{Workload characterization.} Accurate workload modeling is essential for selecting the right cloud database service, configuring resources, and estimating costs. Surveys on AI4DB~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB} discuss workload forecasting using ML, but applying these techniques during the design phase---before production data exists---remains challenging.

\textbf{Data model selection.} Choosing between relational, document, graph, key-value, wide-column, vector, or multi-model databases requires understanding workload characteristics, query patterns, consistency requirements, and cost constraints. No systematic decision framework exists that accounts for all these dimensions~\cite{Lu2019MultiModel}.

\textbf{Compliance-by-design.} Incorporating regulatory requirements (GDPR, HIPAA, data sovereignty) into the initial database design rather than retrofitting compliance is increasingly recognized as essential~\cite{Politou2018GDPR}. This includes data residency constraints, encryption requirements, and audit trail design.

\subsubsection{Deployment phase}
\label{subsubsec:deployment}

The deployment phase covers provider selection, infrastructure configuration, data ingestion, and multi-region setup.

\textbf{Provider selection and vendor lock-in.} Choosing a cloud database provider involves evaluating technical capabilities, pricing, compliance certifications, geographic coverage, and ecosystem integration. Vendor lock-in---through proprietary features, APIs, and data formats---is consistently identified as a top concern across surveys~\cite{Opara2017LockIn,Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Configuration automation.} Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) tools (Terraform, Pulumi, CloudFormation) enable automated database provisioning, but optimal configuration selection---instance types, storage tiers, replication settings, parameter tuning---often requires deep expertise that is difficult to automate~\cite{Zhang2022CDBTune}.

\textbf{Data ingestion and migration.} Initial data loading and ongoing ingestion from diverse sources (operational databases, streaming platforms, file systems) into cloud databases involves ETL/ELT pipeline design, schema mapping, and data quality assurance~\cite{Mathew2021CloudMigration}.

\textbf{Multi-region deployment.} Deploying databases across multiple geographic regions for latency, availability, and compliance reasons introduces challenges in data synchronization, conflict resolution, and region-aware routing~\cite{Corbett2013Spanner}.

\subsubsection{Operation phase}
\label{subsubsec:operation}

The operation phase covers the day-to-day management of running cloud database systems.

\textbf{Monitoring and observability.} Cloud databases generate vast amounts of telemetry data (metrics, logs, traces). Effective monitoring requires understanding normal behavior patterns, detecting anomalies, and correlating events across distributed components~\cite{Ma2021LearnedWorkload}. Surveys on autonomous databases~\cite{Pavlo2017SelfDriving} emphasize the need for ML-driven anomaly detection and root cause analysis.

\textbf{Backup, recovery, and disaster recovery.} While cloud providers automate basic backups (point-in-time recovery, snapshots), disaster recovery across regions, testing recovery procedures, and meeting stringent RPO/RTO targets remain operational challenges~\cite{Verbitski2017}.

\textbf{Security operations.} Ongoing security management includes access control auditing, encryption key rotation, vulnerability patching, network security configuration, and compliance monitoring. The dynamic nature of cloud environments (auto-scaling, serverless) complicates security posture management~\cite{Sanka2022CloudSecSurvey}.

\textbf{Real-time workload management (HTAP).} For HTAP systems~\cite{Haas2024HTAPSurvey}, operational challenges include managing resource contention between transactional and analytical workloads, maintaining freshness guarantees for analytical queries, and preventing analytical queries from degrading OLTP performance.

\subsubsection{Optimization phase}
\label{subsubsec:optimization}

The optimization phase encompasses techniques for improving performance and efficiency of running cloud databases.

\textbf{Learned query optimization.} ML-based query optimizers~\cite{Marcus2021Bao,Zhou2022AI4DB} that learn from workload history to generate better query plans represent a paradigm shift from traditional cost-based optimization. However, deploying learned optimizers in production requires addressing safety (avoiding catastrophic regressions), training efficiency, and adaptability to workload changes.

\textbf{Automated index and materialized view selection.} Cloud databases increasingly offer automated index recommendation (e.g., Azure SQL automatic tuning, Amazon Redshift automatic materialized views), but surveys~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB} report that these systems are often conservative, covering only a fraction of possible optimizations.

\textbf{Knob tuning.} Database configuration parameters (buffer pool size, parallelism degree, checkpoint intervals, etc.) significantly impact performance. ML-based tuning systems~\cite{Zhang2022CDBTune} can explore the high-dimensional configuration space more effectively than manual tuning, but transferring tuning knowledge across different workloads and hardware remains an open problem.

\textbf{Cost--performance trade-off optimization.} In cloud environments, performance optimization cannot be divorced from cost. The optimal configuration minimizes cost subject to performance constraints (or maximizes performance subject to budget constraints), requiring multi-objective optimization~\cite{Leis2023CloudPricing}.

\subsubsection{Evolution phase}
\label{subsubsec:evolution}

The evolution phase addresses long-term changes in database systems, technologies, and requirements.

\textbf{Schema evolution and online DDL.} Changing database schemas in production without downtime (online DDL) is a critical capability for agile development. Cloud-native databases have improved online DDL support, but complex migrations (column type changes, table splits, data backfills) remain challenging~\cite{Li2024CloudNativeSurvey}.

\textbf{Technology migration.} Migrating from one database technology to another (e.g., from a relational database to a document store, or from a legacy system to a cloud-native database) is a major undertaking that surveys identify as poorly supported by existing tools~\cite{Mathew2021CloudMigration}.

\textbf{Adapting to new paradigms.} The emergence of vector databases for AI workloads~\cite{Pan2024VectorDB}, the lakehouse paradigm~\cite{Armbrust2021Lakehouse}, and serverless computing~\cite{Muller2020Serverless} requires existing systems to evolve or be replaced. Understanding when and how to adopt new paradigms is a strategic challenge.

\textbf{LLM integration.} The rapid rise of large language models (LLMs) is creating new requirements for cloud databases, from vector storage and retrieval for RAG pipelines to natural language interfaces for database querying and administration (discussed in detail in Section~\ref{sec:llm}).

\section{Cloud Databases and Large Language Models---A Convergence Case Study}
\label{sec:llm}

The convergence of cloud databases and large language models (LLMs) represents one of the most dynamic and rapidly evolving areas in modern data management. This section examines this convergence as an illustrative case study, paralleling Saeed and Omlin's~\cite{Saeed2023XAI} treatment of XAI in medicine as a domain-specific application.

\subsection{LLM-augmented database interfaces}
\label{subsec:nl2sql}

Natural language interfaces to databases (NLIDB) have been a long-standing research goal. The advent of LLMs has dramatically improved text-to-SQL accuracy, with systems like DIN-SQL~\cite{Pourreza2023DINSQL}, DAIL-SQL~\cite{Gao2023DAILSQL}, and MAC-SQL~\cite{Wang2024MACSQL} achieving over 85\% execution accuracy on the Spider benchmark~\cite{Yu2018Spider}.

\textbf{Current capabilities.} LLM-based text-to-SQL systems leverage schema-aware prompting, few-shot in-context learning, and chain-of-thought reasoning to translate natural language queries into SQL. Cloud vendors are integrating these capabilities: Amazon Q for databases, Azure AI integration with SQL, and Google Duet AI for BigQuery.

\textbf{Open challenges.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Complex queries involving multiple joins, subqueries, and window functions remain error-prone.
    \item Schema understanding for large databases with hundreds of tables and complex relationships.
    \item Multi-turn conversational interfaces that maintain context across query refinements.
    \item Handling ambiguity, domain-specific terminology, and user intent clarification.
    \item Security risks from SQL injection through natural language prompts.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Vector databases as LLM infrastructure}
\label{subsec:vectordb}

Vector databases have emerged as critical infrastructure for LLM applications, particularly retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipelines~\cite{Pan2024VectorDB}.

\textbf{Architecture and workloads.} RAG pipelines embed documents into high-dimensional vectors, store them in vector databases, and retrieve relevant context for LLM prompts based on semantic similarity. This creates unique workload patterns: bulk embedding ingestion, high-throughput approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search, and hybrid queries combining vector similarity with metadata filtering.

\textbf{System landscape.} The vector database market has rapidly diversified, including purpose-built systems (Pinecone, Weaviate, Milvus, Qdrant, Chroma), vector extensions to existing databases (pgvector for PostgreSQL, Elasticsearch vector search), and cloud-native offerings (Amazon OpenSearch vector engine, Azure AI Search, Google Vertex AI Vector Search)~\cite{Pan2024VectorDB}.

\textbf{Open challenges.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Scalability and freshness---keeping vector indexes up-to-date as source documents change.
    \item Multi-modal vector search combining text, image, and structured metadata.
    \item Quality metrics for retrieval that correlate with downstream LLM task performance.
    \item Cost optimization for storing and querying billions of high-dimensional vectors.
    \item Integration with existing cloud database ecosystems (transactions, access control, governance).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{LLMs for database administration}
\label{subsec:llmdba}

LLMs are increasingly applied to automate database administration tasks, including performance diagnosis, query optimization, and anomaly resolution.

\textbf{Systems and approaches.} D-Bot~\cite{Zhou2024DBot} uses LLMs for database diagnosis, performing root cause analysis of performance anomalies by querying system views and analyzing metrics. DB-GPT~\cite{Xue2024DBGPT} provides an LLM-powered framework for database interaction, supporting text-to-SQL, data analysis, and administration tasks. GPTuner~\cite{Lao2024GPTuner} leverages LLMs for database knob tuning, combining GPT-4 knowledge with Bayesian optimization.

\textbf{Open challenges.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reliability---LLMs can hallucinate incorrect diagnostic conclusions or generate harmful administration commands.
    \item Safety guardrails---preventing LLM-driven administration from executing destructive operations.
    \item Integrating LLM reasoning with structured database knowledge (system catalogs, performance models).
    \item Evaluation frameworks for LLM-based DBA tools that measure real-world effectiveness.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Challenges and open problems at the convergence}
\label{subsec:llmchallenges}

The LLM--database convergence creates several overarching challenges:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Infrastructure co-design.} Jointly optimizing cloud database infrastructure for both traditional workloads and LLM-related workloads (embedding generation, vector search, context retrieval) requires new architectural thinking.

    \item \textbf{Data governance for AI.} Ensuring that LLM applications respect data access policies, privacy regulations, and audit requirements when retrieving context from cloud databases.

    \item \textbf{Evaluation and benchmarking.} Standardized benchmarks for vector database performance, text-to-SQL accuracy on real-world schemas, and LLM-based DBA effectiveness are needed.

    \item \textbf{Cost management.} LLM API calls combined with cloud database queries create complex cost structures that are difficult to predict and optimize.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Short-term directions (1--3 years)}
\label{subsec:shortterm}

\textbf{Cloud-native database benchmarks.} Developing comprehensive benchmarks that capture cloud-specific characteristics---elasticity, multi-tenancy, disaggregated storage, pricing---is an immediate priority. These benchmarks should extend established suites (TPC, YCSB) with cloud-native metrics and enable fair cross-provider comparison~\cite{Rabl2019BenchmarkSurvey}.

\textbf{Production-ready learned optimizers.} Transitioning learned query optimization~\cite{Marcus2021Bao}, learned indexes~\cite{Kraska2018LearnedIndex}, and ML-based knob tuning~\cite{Zhang2022CDBTune} from research prototypes to production-deployable systems requires solving safety, robustness, and maintenance challenges. Hybrid approaches that combine ML with traditional optimization offer a pragmatic path forward~\cite{Zhou2022AI4DB}.

\textbf{Vector database maturity.} The vector database ecosystem needs standardization in APIs, indexing algorithms, quality metrics, and hybrid search capabilities. Integration with existing cloud database governance and management infrastructure is essential for enterprise adoption~\cite{Pan2024VectorDB}.

\textbf{Reliable text-to-SQL.} Improving text-to-SQL systems for real-world enterprise schemas (large, complex, poorly documented) and establishing evaluation protocols beyond academic benchmarks are near-term priorities~\cite{Yu2018Spider}.

\subsection{Medium-term directions (3--5 years)}
\label{subsec:mediumterm}

\textbf{Autonomous databases with explainability.} As databases become more autonomous, the ability to explain automated decisions (why an index was created, why a query plan was chosen, why a knob was changed) becomes critical for DBA trust and debugging. Connecting AI4DB with XAI research~\cite{Saeed2023XAI} is a fertile direction.

\textbf{Cross-cloud middleware and portability.} Developing middleware layers, standardized APIs, and portable data formats that enable seamless operation across multiple cloud providers would reduce vendor lock-in and enable competitive pressure to drive down costs~\cite{Opara2017LockIn}.

\textbf{Edge--cloud database federation.} Building database federation frameworks that seamlessly span edge devices, fog nodes, and cloud backends---with appropriate consistency, latency, and privacy trade-offs---will be essential for next-generation IoT and autonomous systems~\cite{Gomes2024EdgeFog}.

\textbf{Privacy-preserving cloud analytics.} Practical deployment of privacy-enhancing technologies (differential privacy, homomorphic encryption, secure computation) in cloud databases for real-world analytical workloads at acceptable performance levels~\cite{Acar2018HE}.

\subsection{Long-term directions (5+ years)}
\label{subsec:longterm}

\textbf{Intent-driven databases.} Moving from declarative query languages (SQL) to \emph{intent-driven} interfaces where users express what they need in natural language or high-level objectives, and the database autonomously determines the optimal data model, schema, query plan, and execution strategy.

\textbf{Carbon-neutral database operations.} Designing cloud database systems that achieve carbon neutrality through energy-proportional computing, carbon-aware workload scheduling, and integration with renewable energy sources~\cite{IEA2024DataCenters}.

\textbf{Unified multi-model platforms.} Converging relational, graph, document, vector, time-series, and streaming data models into truly unified platforms with a single query language, consistent transactional semantics, and integrated optimization~\cite{Lu2019MultiModel}.

\textbf{Self-evolving schemas.} Databases that autonomously evolve their schemas in response to changing workload patterns, new data sources, and evolving application requirements, guided by knowledge engineering principles and formal schema evolution theories.

\section{Final Remarks}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper has presented the first systematic meta-survey of cloud database research, synthesizing findings from 58 survey papers published between 2019 and 2025. By applying a rigorous systematic literature review methodology, the scattered landscape of cloud database challenges has been consolidated into a structured, dual-theme taxonomy comprising eighteen cross-cutting challenges and five lifecycle-phase challenges.

The analysis reveals several key insights. First, the cloud database field is remarkably diverse, spanning architectural paradigms (cloud-native, disaggregated, serverless), data models (relational, document, graph, vector, multi-model), processing modes (OLTP, OLAP, HTAP, streaming), and deployment contexts (single-region, multi-region, edge--cloud). No single survey captures this full breadth, underscoring the value of a meta-level synthesis.

Second, certain challenges recur across nearly all surveyed domains: \emph{scalability and elasticity}, \emph{consistency trade-offs}, \emph{security and privacy}, \emph{cost optimization}, and \emph{AI--database convergence} are identified as open problems in the majority of the 58 surveys. This convergence of concerns suggests that progress on these foundational challenges would benefit the entire field.

Third, the convergence of cloud databases with large language models is creating a new class of challenges and opportunities that span traditional database research boundaries. Vector databases, text-to-SQL, and LLM-based database administration are rapidly moving from research to practice, but significant gaps in reliability, governance, and evaluation remain.

Fourth, from a knowledge engineering perspective, cloud databases present rich opportunities for applying DKE techniques: knowledge graphs for metadata management, ontology-mediated data integration, formal models for consistency and cost reasoning, and knowledge-based approaches to schema evolution and migration.

This meta-survey is intended to serve three purposes: (1)~as a comprehensive reference for researchers entering or working in the cloud database space, providing a structured map of the territory and its open problems; (2)~as a strategic guide for practitioners navigating cloud database technology selection, deployment, and evolution; and (3)~as a bridge between the cloud database community and the data and knowledge engineering community, highlighting opportunities for cross-pollination.
